Durga Puja Project

INTRODUCTION

Durga Puja is a major Hindu festival that is celebrated in India and Bangladesh with great enthusiasm and devotion. The festival is celebrated in honor of the goddess Durga, who is considered to be the mother of the universe and the embodiment of power and strength. Durga Puja is celebrated during the Hindu month of Ashwin, which typically falls between September and October in the Gregorian calendar.

The festival of Durga Puja is celebrated for ten days, with the main celebrations taking place on the last five days. During these five days, elaborate rituals and ceremonies are performed in temples and homes to invoke the blessings of the goddess. One of the main attractions of the festival is the creation of large, colorful and intricate idols of the goddess, which are placed in pandals (temporary structures) for the public to view and worship.


9 DAYS OF PUJA

The first day of Durga Puja is known as Mahalaya, which marks the beginning of the festival and the arrival of the goddess on earth. On this day, devotees wake up early in the morning and take a dip in holy rivers or lakes to purify themselves. They then perform various rituals and offer prayers to the goddess.

The next three days, known as Shashthi, Saptami, and Ashtami, are dedicated to the worship of the goddess. On these days, devotees offer prayers, perform puja (worship), and sing devotional songs in the goddess's honor. The main attraction of these days is the elaborate puja rituals performed by priests in temples and homes.

The ninth day, known as Navami, is the final day of the festival and is considered the most auspicious day for the worship of the goddess. On this day, devotees perform the Sandhi Puja, which is a special puja performed at the juncture of Ashtami and Navami.

The tenth day, known as Vijayadashami, marks the end of the festival. On this day, the idols of the goddess are taken out in a grand procession and immersed in a nearby river or lake. This signifies the goddess's return to her heavenly abode.

Durga Puja is not just a religious festival but it is also a cultural and social event that brings people together. The festival is celebrated with great enthusiasm and joy, and people from all walks of life participate in the celebrations. It is a time for family and friends to come together and celebrate the victory of good over evil.



CONCLUSION

In conclusion, Durga Puja is a major Hindu festival that is celebrated with great devotion and enthusiasm in India and Bangladesh. It is a celebration of the goddess Durga and her victory over evil. The festival is a time for family and friends to come together and enjoy the cultural and social aspects of the festival. It is a time for people to reflect on the values of good over evil and the importance of standing up for what is right.
